Every rescue coordinator knows the spreadsheet maze. One sheet for fosters. One for animals in foster. One for animals needing foster. A fourth for medical holds. A fifth for... something nobody remembers.

Foster coordination is hard because it involves managing relationships between animals and people across time, with status that changes frequently and urgently.

## What makes foster tracking hard

**Capacity is dynamic.** A foster who had room for two cats last month is now full because their kid is having surgery. A new foster approved last week can take a dog but not cats.

**Animals move.** A foster placement isn't a static assignment — it has a start date, a likely end date, a current status, and sometimes an unexpected change.

**Communication is continuous.** Coordinators need to know which fosters haven't checked in, which animals are coming up on their 30-day review, and which fosters are ready for another placement.

**Errors are costly.** Sending an animal to a foster who isn't ready, or losing track of a placement, creates real problems for the animal.

## A better model

Open Paw models foster coordination around a few clean concepts:

**Foster status on the person record.** Is this foster currently active? What species can they take? What's their current capacity? What's their last check-in?

**Placement records tied to animals.** Each animal in foster has a placement record: which foster, what dates, what status.

**Task integration.** When a placement is approaching its review date, a task is automatically created. No tracking it manually.

**Clear capacity view.** The coordinator can see, at a glance, which fosters have open capacity and what type.

## The practical workflow

When a new animal needs a foster:

1. Check the capacity view for fosters with openings matching this animal's species.
2. Filter by geography if placement distance matters.
3. Review the foster's notes and history.
4. Assign the placement.
5. Set a check-in reminder.

When a placement ends:

1. Mark the placement complete.
2. Update the animal's status.
3. Note whether the foster is ready for another placement.

None of this requires a spreadsheet. It requires clear data structure and a UI that doesn't make you think.
