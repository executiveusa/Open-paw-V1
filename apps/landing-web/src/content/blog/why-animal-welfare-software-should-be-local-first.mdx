Most software sold to animal welfare organizations follows the same pattern: create an account, pay a monthly subscription, store your data on someone else's server, and hope they stay in business.

For large, well-funded shelters, this works fine. For the small rescue running on volunteer hours and donation jars, it creates real problems.

## What local-first means

Local-first software stores your primary data on your own machine or local network. The application works without an internet connection. You can back up your own data. You own the files.

This is how software used to work before everything became a cloud service.

## Why it matters for rescues

**Rescue teams often work in low-connectivity environments.** A foster coordinator in a rural county may have spotty cell service. A field volunteer at an intake event may have no connection at all. Local-first software keeps working.

**Small organizations can't absorb unexpected price increases.** When your monthly software bill doubles, a large shelter absorbs it. A small rescue might have to choose between software and supplies.

**Data portability is a survival skill.** Software companies shut down. Products get acquired. When that happens, cloud-only customers often lose access to their own records. Local-first users just keep their files.

**Sensitive data should be controlled.** Animal records sometimes contain information about abuse cases, owner disputes, or animals in legal proceedings. Who has access to that data matters.

## The tradeoff

Local-first software does require more setup than a cloud subscription. There's no automatic sync across devices without configuration. Sharing records across a distributed team takes more planning.

Open Paw is designed to make this tradeoff worthwhile. The default is simple: one machine, one database, full functionality. When you need more, there's a path to local network sharing and self-hosted cloud deployment.

## The conclusion

Not every organization needs local-first software. But the organizations that need it most — small rescues, volunteer-run foster networks, rural animal control programs — are often the least well-served by cloud-only tools.
