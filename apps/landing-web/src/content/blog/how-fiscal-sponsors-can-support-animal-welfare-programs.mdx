Fiscal sponsorship is one of the most underappreciated models in the nonprofit world — and in animal welfare specifically.

A fiscal sponsor is an existing 501(c)(3) that extends its legal and tax status to programs that either can't or don't want to incorporate independently. This lets a rescue operating informally access grant funding, accept tax-deductible donations, and operate under a more established organization's umbrella.

## Why fiscal sponsorship matters for animal welfare

Starting a formal nonprofit is a significant administrative burden. Filing for 501(c)(3) status takes time and often money. Maintaining it requires annual filings, board governance, and accounting.

Many effective rescue efforts are run by individuals or small groups who do excellent animal welfare work but don't have the infrastructure for formal nonprofit status. Fiscal sponsorship lets them access the resources that formal status provides without the full overhead.

## The coordination problem

When a fiscal sponsor supports multiple animal welfare programs, the coordination requirements multiply. Each sponsored program needs:

- Separate animal records (their animals, their fosters, their adopters)
- Separate grant tracking (their funders, their applications)
- Role-appropriate access (volunteers of Program A shouldn't see Program B's records)
- Consolidated reporting (the sponsor organization needs aggregate impact metrics)

This is a multi-tenant problem. Each program needs its own isolated data, but the sponsor organization needs visibility across all programs.

## How Open Paw models this

Open Paw's tenant model is designed with fiscal sponsorship in mind. Each program runs as a separate tenant. Records, grants, people, and animals are isolated by tenant.

The sponsor organization can hold a parent tenant view with read access to aggregate metrics — animal outcomes, grant activity, impact data — across sponsored programs, without accessing individual case details.

Roles work at the tenant level. A volunteer in Program A has no access to Program B's records.

## The grant implication

Fiscal sponsors often hold relationships with funders that individual programs don't have. The sponsor can apply for grants on behalf of multiple programs, then sub-grant internally.

Funding Radar is designed to track this. A grant can be attributed to the sponsor organization but linked to the program it funds. Reporting obligations flow to the appropriate program team.

## The practical benefit

This model lets small but effective rescue programs focus on the animals rather than the administrative infrastructure. The fiscal sponsor holds the legal and financial structure. The programs hold the mission.

Open Paw is designed to support both sides of this relationship.
