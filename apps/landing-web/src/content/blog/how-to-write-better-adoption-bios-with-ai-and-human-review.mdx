Adoption bios are one of the most underrated parts of shelter operations. A good bio does real work: it surfaces the right match, sets accurate expectations, and gets pets adopted faster.

Writing them well is hard, especially when you have 30 animals and a volunteer coordinator working weekends.

## Why most adoption bios underperform

Most adoption bios fall into one of two failure modes.

**Too vague.** "Fluffy is a sweet dog who loves people and would make a great family pet." This describes nearly every dog in the shelter. It doesn't help the right family find the right dog.

**Too clinical.** "Labrador mix, 3 years, male, neutered. Up to date on vaccines. Heartworm negative." This is a medical record, not a bio. It doesn't make anyone want to meet the dog.

The best bios are specific, honest, and read like they were written by someone who has actually spent time with the animal.

## Where AI can help

AI can accelerate the drafting process. A coordinator can spend 5 minutes writing notes about an animal — personality, quirks, what they need in a home, what they love — and get a structured, readable bio draft back in seconds.

This is genuinely useful because writing is the bottleneck, not knowledge. Coordinators who know every animal well often struggle to sit down and write a compelling bio for each one.

## Where AI requires human review

AI bios require careful review before publication. Common issues:

**Fabricated behaviors.** An AI will occasionally invent a detail that sounds plausible but isn't true. "Luna loves to swim" — does she? The human who knows the animal must verify every specific claim.

**Inappropriate positivity.** Some animals have behavioral challenges that need to be disclosed honestly. An AI will tend to minimize or omit these. Honest disclosure protects both the animal and the adopter.

**Wrong tone.** A serious working dog doesn't need the same register as a playful puppy. AI sometimes flattens personality into generic warmth.

## The Open Paw workflow

In Open Paw, the AI bio drafting tool works like this:

1. Fill in the animal's personality notes, quirks, and needs.
2. Run the bio draft generator.
3. Review the output. Correct factual errors. Add specific details. Disclose behavioral needs honestly.
4. Save the approved bio to the animal's public profile.

The AI output is always clearly marked as a draft until a human approves it. Unapproved drafts don't appear on public adoption pages.

This keeps the workflow fast without letting automation substitute for human judgment about an individual animal.
